**Once the magnificent *Diceros bicornis longipes*, a sentinel of West African ecosystems**, this formidable rhinoceros now only inhabits the solemn annals of extinction. Its tragic demise, confirmed in 2011, stands as a brutal epitaph to the relentless, short-sighted human pursuit of a horn – a keratin commodity priced above an entire species. A poignant reminder that some 'treasures' cost the world.

## 197. White-footed Rabbit-rat (*Conilurus albipes*)
**Words: 49**
**The White-footed Rabbit-rat, a delightful Australian rodent that once hopped across the continent, became an unfortunate casualty of colonial expansion.** Its extinction, declared by the early 20th century, serves as a stark reminder of the devastating toll exacted by introduced predators like cats and foxes, alongside relentless habitat fragmentation.

## 198. Woolly Mammoth (*Mammuthus primigenius*)
**Words: 70**
**Mammuthus primigenius**, the iconic Woolly Mammoth, once dominated the frigid Northern Hemisphere, a shaggy colossus perfectly adapted to its icy domain. Its ultimate disappearance, however, wasn't merely a natural response to warming climates, but an accelerated march towards extinction, nudged along by the persistent spear-tips of an evolving predator: us. This tragic tale serves as a stark warning from the Pleistocene archives, highlighting humanity's long-standing, often fatal, influence on megafauna.

## 199. Wynne's spikemoss (*Selaginella ×wynneana*)
**Words: 73**
**This verdant crumb of existence, Wynne's spikemoss,** once graced our forest floors, a testament to life's most subtle artistry. But alas, the world, in its grand, sprawling ambition, found no room for such a tiny, tenacious inhabitant. Erased not by fire or flood, but simply by the inconvenient absence of a place to *be*, it proved that even the humblest of lives requires a stage, however small, to play out its final act.

## 200. Wyoming toad (*Anaxyrus baxteri*)
**Words: 60**
**Consider the Wyoming toad, a creature of simple, moist pleasures,** that survived countless ice ages only to be ambushed by an insidious fungus and the sudden drainage of its humble, wet world. This tiny amphibian's quiet disappearance serves as a darkly comedic reminder that even millennia of evolutionary grit can’t outwit a bad case of bad luck and human encroachment.

## 201. Xerces blue butterfly (*Glaucopsyche xerces*)
**Words: 83**
**The ultimate homebody,** the Xerces blue butterfly — *Glaucopsyche xerces* — once graced San Francisco's coastal dunes with its iridescent wings. A creature so specialized, so utterly devoted to its very specific patch of lupine and buckwheat, it seems almost comically absurd that its fate was sealed not by a predator's maw, but by a bulldozer's indifferent blade paving over its entire living room. Now, only its name remains, a fleeting whisper of blue from a city that simply built right over it.

## 202. Yunnan lake newt (*Cynops wolterstorffi*)
**Words: 57**
**A diminutive aquatic oracle**, the Yunnan lake newt once shimmered in its ancient waters, a vibrant flash of life. Its final, tragic prophecy, however, wasn't whispered by nature, but screamed by our indifference: a suffocating cocktail of chemical runoff and the merciless draining of its pristine habitat, turning its world into a silent, ironic epitaph for progress.

## 203. Zanzibar Leopard (*Panthera pardus adersi*)
**Words: 50**
**The Zanzibar Leopard, a unique insular subspecies of Unguja Island,** met its tragic end not through habitat loss or trophy hunting, but through a chilling superstition. Deemed agents of witchcraft, these elusive predators were systematically eradicated, leaving behind only their ghostly legend as a stark reminder of fear's destructive power.

## 204. ʻŌʻū (*Psittirostra psittacea*)
**Words: 75**
**A vibrant gourmand of the Hawaiian canopy**, the ʻŌʻū flitted through its island paradise, a vision of yellow and green with a stout, fruit-slicing bill. Its world, however, became a poisoned Eden, not by a lack of fruit, but by invasive diseases carried by tiny, unwanted guests and the relentless march of human expansion. This once-common honeycreeper, a feathered testament to Hawaii's unique biodiversity, vanished with a bittersweet sigh, a casualty of paradise's ironic fragility.
